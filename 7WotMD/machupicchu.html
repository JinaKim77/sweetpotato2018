About

Machu Picchu is a 15th-century Inca citadel. It is located in the Cuzco Region in Peru, 2,430 meters above sea level. It was built using polished dry-stone walls, which were typical of Inca style. The citadel has three primary structures: the Intihuatana (a ritual stone), the Temple of the Sun and the Room of the Three Windows. By 1976, 30% of Machu Picchu has been restored. In 1981, Machu Picchu was declared a Peruvian Historic Sanctuary, as well as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983. Since 2007, it is one of the Seven Wonders of the Modern World.

History

Machu Picchu was built around 1450 during the ruling period of 2 Inca rulers, Pachacutec Inca Yupanqui and Tupac Inca Yupanqui. It is belived that the citadel was ordered to be built by Pachacutec for himself after a successful military campaign.


It is estimated that Machu Picchu was occupied by about 750 people at one time, with that number dropping to roughly 100 people during the harsher seasons who focused mainly on maintenance.


A lot of farming in Machu Picchu was done on the numerous man-made terraces there. Study of the soil showed that the main crops grown were corn and potatoes. However this was not enough to support the ~750 people living at the citadel, hence much of what they ate was imported from the surrounding valleys.


Machu Picchu was abandoned around 1570 due to Spanish Conquests in other parts of the Inca Empire.
